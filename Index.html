{/* Tenses */}
Topic

Tenses

Past, Present, Future forms

What are Tenses?

Tenses tell us WHEN an action happens - in the past, present, or future.

Present Tense:

  • Simple Present: I eat / He eats (daily actions)
  • Present Continuous: I am eating (happening now)
  • Present Perfect: I have eaten (completed recently)

Past Tense:

  • Simple Past: I ate (completed action)
  • Past Continuous: I was eating (was happening)
  • Past Perfect: I had eaten (completed before another past action)

Future Tense:

  • Simple Future: I will eat (will happen)
  • Future Continuous: I will be eating (will be happening)

Quick Tip:

Look for time words: yesterday (past), now/today (present), tomorrow (future). Practice by writing 5 sentences in each tense about your daily routine.

{/* Active Passive Voice */}
Topic

Active and Passive Voice

Changing sentence structure

Active Voice:

Subject does the action

Example: Ram writes a letter.

Passive Voice:

Action is done to the subject

Example: A letter is written by Ram.

How to Convert:

Step 1: Find the object in active sentence → It becomes subject in passive

Step 2: Use correct form of "be" + past participle of verb

Step 3: Add "by" + original subject (optional)

Exam Tip:

Always comes in exams! Remember: Object becomes subject. Learn past participles of common verbs (write-written, do-done, see-seen).

Ch 1

Real Numbers

Euclid's Division, HCF, LCM, Prime numbers

What are Real Numbers?

Real numbers are all numbers you can think of - positive, negative, fractions, decimals. Example: 5, -3, 2.5, 0, √2 are all real numbers.

Key Topics:

  • Euclid's Division Algorithm: A method to find HCF of two numbers
  • HCF & LCM: Highest Common Factor and Lowest Common Multiple
  • Decimal Expansion: Terminating and Non-terminating decimals

Example:

Find HCF of 18 and 24

Step 1: Write factors of both numbers
18 = 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
24 = 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24

Step 2: Find common factors = 1, 2, 3, 6

Answer: HCF = 6 (highest common factor)

Exam Tip:

This chapter has many formula-based questions. Practice finding HCF using both methods - listing factors and Euclid's algorithm. Remember: HCF × LCM = Product of two numbers.

{/* Chapter 2 */}
Ch 2

Polynomials

Linear, Quadratic, Cubic polynomials

What are Polynomials?

Polynomials are expressions with variables and powers. Example: 2x + 3, x² + 5x + 6. Think of them as math sentences with x (unknown) that we need to solve.

Key Topics:

  • Types of Polynomials: Linear (x), Quadratic (x²), Cubic (x³)
  • Zeroes of Polynomial: Values of x where polynomial becomes zero
  • Division Algorithm: Dividing one polynomial by another

Example:

Find zeroes of polynomial x² - 5x + 6

Step 1: Factorize: x² - 5x + 6 = (x - 2)(x - 3)

Step 2: Set each factor to zero
x - 2 = 0, so x = 2
x - 3 = 0, so x = 3

Answer: Zeroes are 2 and 3

Exam Tip:

Factorization questions are very common. Practice finding zeroes and verifying using sum and product relationships. Don't forget to check your answer by substituting back.

{/* Chapter 3 */}
Ch 3

Pair of Linear Equations in Two Variables

Graphical & Algebraic methods

What are Linear Equations?

These are equations with two unknowns (x and y). Example: 2x + 3y = 12. We need to find values of both x and y that satisfy the equation.

Key Topics:

  • Graphical Method: Drawing lines and finding intersection point
  • Substitution Method: Find one variable, then substitute
  • Elimination Method: Add or subtract equations to eliminate a variable

Example:

Solve: x + y = 7 and x - y = 3

Using Elimination:
Add both equations: (x + y) + (x - y) = 7 + 3
2x = 10, so x = 5

Put x = 5 in first equation: 5 + y = 7
So y = 2

Answer: x = 5, y = 2

Exam Tip:

Word problems are common in exams. Read carefully and convert sentences into equations. Practice all three methods - you may need to use different ones for different questions.

{/* Chapter 4 */}
Ch 4

Quadratic Equations

Solving equations with x²

What are Quadratic Equations?

Equations where highest power of x is 2. Standard form: ax² + bx + c = 0. Example: x² - 5x + 6 = 0. These have two solutions.

Important Formulas:

  • Quadratic Formula: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a
  • Discriminant: D = b² - 4ac (tells us about nature of roots)
  • Sum of roots: α + β = -b/a
  • Product of roots: αβ = c/a

Exam Tip:

This is a HIGH SCORING chapter. Memorize the quadratic formula and discriminant. Practice word problems about age, speed, and area - they always come in exams.

{/* Chapter 5 */}
Ch 5

Arithmetic Progressions (AP)

Number sequences with patterns

What is AP?

A sequence where each number is obtained by adding a fixed number to the previous one. Example: 2, 5, 8, 11, 14... (adding 3 each time)

Important Formulas:

  • nth term: aₙ = a + (n-1)d
  • Sum of n terms: Sₙ = n/2 [2a + (n-1)d]
  • Alternative sum: Sₙ = n/2 (first + last term)

Where: a = first term, d = common difference, n = number of terms

Exam Tip:

Easy chapter to score full marks! Just memorize the 2-3 formulas. Practice finding d (common difference) first - it's the key to solving most problems.

If D = 0: Real and equal roots

If D {"<"} 0: No real roots (imaginary)

Mirrors, Lenses, Ray diagrams

What is Light?

Light is energy that allows us to see. It travels in straight lines and can bounce off surfaces (reflection) or bend when passing through different materials (refraction).

Key Topics:

  • Reflection: Light bouncing off mirrors (plane, concave, convex)
  • Refraction: Light bending when passing through glass, water
  • Lenses: Convex (converging) and Concave (diverging) lenses

Important Formulas:

Mirror Formula: 1/f = 1/v + 1/u

Magnification: m = -v/u = h'/h

Lens Formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u

f = focal length, v = image distance, u = object distance

Exam Tip:

Ray diagrams are VERY important. Practice drawing them for all cases. Remember sign convention: distances measured from pole, against incident ray are negative.

{/* Chapter 11 - Human Eye */}
Ch 11

Human Eye and Colourful World

Vision, Defects, Spectrum, Rainbow

How do we see?

Our eye works like a camera. Light enters through the cornea, passes through the lens which focuses it on the retina (like a screen). The brain interprets this as images.

Key Topics:

  • Parts of Eye: Cornea, Iris, Pupil, Lens, Retina
  • Defects: Myopia (near-sightedness), Hypermetropia (far-sightedness)
  • Dispersion: White light splitting into 7 colors (VIBGYOR)

Exam Tip:

Diagram of eye is frequently asked. Know which lens corrects which defect: Concave for Myopia, Convex for Hypermetropia. Atmospheric refraction explains twinkling of stars.

{/* Chapter 12 - Electricity */}
Ch 12

Electricity

Current, Voltage, Resistance, Circuits

What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electric charge (electrons) through a conductor like a wire. Think of it like water flowing through a pipe.

Important Formulas:

  • Ohm's Law: V = I × R
  • Power: P = V × I = I²R = V²/R
  • Electrical Energy: E = P × t
  • Series Resistance: R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Exam Tip:

HIGH SCORING chapter with lots of numerical problems. Memorize all formulas. Practice circuit problems - both series and parallel. Know the units: Voltage (V), Current (A), Resistance (Ω).

Chemical Reactions and Equations

Types of reactions, Balancing equations

What is a Chemical Reaction?

When substances react and change into different substances. Example: Burning wood converts it to ash and smoke. Iron rusting is also a chemical reaction.

Types of Reactions:

  • Combination: Two substances join to form one (A + B → AB)
  • Decomposition: One substance breaks into two (AB → A + B)
  • Displacement: One element replaces another (A + BC → AC + B)
  • Redox: Oxidation (gain of oxygen) & Reduction (loss of oxygen)

Exam Tip:

Balancing equations is VERY IMPORTANT. Practice writing and balancing at least 20 equations. Remember: atoms on left side = atoms on right side.

Acids, Bases and Salts

pH scale, Indicators, Neutralization

Simple Explanation

Acids: Sour taste (lemon, vinegar). Turn blue litmus red.
Bases: Bitter taste, soapy feel. Turn red litmus blue.
Salts: Formed when acid reacts with base.

Key Concepts:

  • pH Scale: 0-14 scale. Less than 7 = Acid, 7 = Neutral, More than 7 = Base
  • Neutralization: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
  • Examples: HCl (acid), NaOH (base), NaCl (common salt)

Exam Tip:

Know common acids and bases with their chemical formulas. pH values are frequently asked. Practice writing neutralization reactions with proper equations.

Life Processes

Nutrition, Respiration, Transport, Excretion

What are Life Processes?

Basic functions that keep living organisms alive. All living things need food, breathe, remove waste, and transport materials inside their body.

Main Life Processes:

  • Nutrition: How organisms get food (autotrophic & heterotrophic)
  • Respiration: Breaking down food to release energy (aerobic & anaerobic)
  • Transportation: Moving materials around the body (blood, xylem, phloem)
  • Excretion: Removing waste products from body (kidneys, lungs)

What is Reproduction?

Process by which living organisms produce new individuals of their own kind. This ensures continuation of species.

  • Asexual Reproduction: Single parent (fission, budding, fragmentation)
  • Sexual Reproduction: Two parents, involves male and female gametes
  • Reproduction in Plants: Vegetative propagation, flowers, pollination

Exam Tip:

Diagrams of flower parts and human reproductive system are frequently asked. Know differences between asexual and sexual reproduction. Understand methods of contraception.
